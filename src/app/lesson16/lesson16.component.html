Lesson 16: Controlling I

1.0 Objectives

  • Explain the meaning of the controlling process.
  • Describe the characteristics of controlling.
  • Identify and explain the types of controls.

1.1 Introduction

Controlling is one of the most crucial functions of management, integral to achieving organizational goals. It is not just about monitoring but involves guiding, supervising, and aligning activities with predetermined plans. Effective controlling enables managers to measure progress, identify deviations, and take corrective actions to ensure the desired outcomes.

The Role of Leadership in Controlling

Leadership plays a pivotal role in the controlling process. A good leader not only provides direction but also supervises and monitors the activities being carried out by subordinates. By keeping track of these activities, the leader ensures that the organization is moving in the right direction.

Definition of Controlling

Controlling is a managerial function aimed at ensuring activities are performed as per the plans, thereby contributing to the organization’s goals. It ensures efficient and effective utilization of resources while minimizing deviations.

Experts' Definitions of Controlling

  • Henri Fayol: "Control of an undertaking consists of seeing that everything is being carried out in accordance with the plan which has been adopted, the orders which have been given, and the principles which have been laid down. Its object is to point out mistakes in order that they may be rectified and prevented from recurring."
  • Brech: "Control is checking current performance against predetermined standards contained in the plans, with a view to ensure adequate progress and satisfactory performance, and also recording the experience gained from the working of these plans as a guide to possible future needs."
  • Harold Koontz: "Controlling is the measurement and correction of performance in order to make sure that enterprise objectives and the plans devised to attain them are accomplished."

The Nature of Controlling

Controlling is often aligned with the directing function of management, as it involves supervision and guidance. Unlike the general perception of controlling as a method to restrict or prohibit activities, managerial controlling focuses on improving performance and achieving objectives.

Controlling as a Dynamic Process

Control involves analyzing performance and ensuring alignment with organizational goals. It’s about identifying gaps and reducing them.

Controlling vs. Supervising

While controlling and supervising are related, controlling is broader. Supervision focuses on guiding individuals, whereas controlling includes measuring performance, analyzing results, and taking corrective actions.

Conclusion: Controlling is not about restricting actions but about facilitating task completion. It ensures organizational goals are met efficiently and effectively.

1.2 Characteristics of Controlling

1. Forward-Looking

Controlling is inherently future-oriented. It focuses on forecasting potential issues and aligning actions to prevent them.

  • A school evaluating its results and improving future performance.
  • A company using market research to adjust production schedules based on demand.

2. Continuous Process

Controlling is an ongoing process. It ensures objectives are consistently met through continuous monitoring and evaluation.

  • A production manager tracking daily output to meet targets.
  • A principal reviewing lesson plans regularly to ensure alignment with curriculum.

3. Coordinated and Integrated System

Effective controlling requires coordination across various departments to ensure organizational goals are met.

  • In an educational institution, different departments must work together to ensure smooth operations.
  • In manufacturing, procurement, production, and sales must be aligned to meet targets.

4. Starts at Top Management

Controlling begins at the top management level and cascades downward to ensure alignment with organizational goals.

  • A CEO sets financial targets, and middle managers ensure progress.
  • In a national campaign, policymakers introduce initiatives like the "Ladali Scheme" to improve education.

5. Performance Measurement and Feedback

Controlling involves measuring actual performance against established standards and using feedback for decision-making.

  • Customer feedback is analyzed after product launches to adjust marketing strategies.
  • Teachers use student results to evaluate and modify teaching methods.

6. Emphasis on Rationality and Objectivity

Effective controlling relies on objective criteria and data-driven analysis for decision-making.

  • Retail chains use sales data to assess store performance.
  • Hospitals compare recovery rates against industry benchmarks to evaluate care quality.

7. Corrective and Adaptive Nature

Controlling identifies deviations and takes corrective actions to address them, adapting strategies as needed.

  • A company switches suppliers to address supply chain disruptions.
  • A school adopts digital learning techniques if traditional methods fail to engage students.

8. Goal-Oriented Function

Controlling ensures activities contribute to organizational goals and objectives.

  • A nonprofit organization tracks fundraising efforts to allocate resources to community programs.
  • A government agency monitors public policy implementation to meet socio-economic goals.

Conclusion: The characteristics of controlling—its forward-looking nature, continuity, coordination, and top-level initiation—highlight its vital role in ensuring organizational goals are achieved efficiently.

1.3 Types of Control

1.3.1 Feed Forward Control

Feed forward control anticipates and prevents potential deviations before they occur.

  • Ensuring raw materials meet quality standards before production.
  • Induction training programs to familiarize employees with policies.

1.3.2 Concurrent Controls

Concurrent controls monitor ongoing activities in real-time and enable immediate corrective actions.

  • Supervisors monitor safety protocols on the assembly line.
  • IT teams track network performance in real-time to prevent downtime.

1.3.3 Feedback Controls

Feedback controls evaluate the outcomes of processes and use the information to improve future performance.

  • Conducting customer satisfaction surveys after a product launch.
  • Reviewing student exam results to adjust teaching methods for future terms.

Integration of Control Types

In practice, organizations often use a combination of feed forward, concurrent, and feedback controls to ensure comprehensive oversight.

1.4 Summary

  • Controlling ensures activities are performed as planned to meet organizational goals.
  • It involves a continuous process of monitoring, evaluating, and adjusting activities.
  • Different types of control—feed forward, concurrent, and feedback—are used to improve efficiency and quality.

Challenges in Controlling: Resistance to change, over-control, and inaccurate data can hinder effectiveness. These challenges can be overcome with effective communication, balanced oversight, and investment in technology.
