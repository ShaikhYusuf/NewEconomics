Lesson 27: Equity Theory of Motivation

2. Introduction

The Equity Theory of Motivation, developed by John Adams in the 1960s, focuses on the role of fairness in motivation within workplace relationships. The theory posits that people are motivated by fairness in social exchanges, particularly the relationship between the input they contribute and the outcomes they receive in return. If employees perceive a fair exchange, they are likely to feel satisfied and motivated to continue their work. Conversely, if they perceive inequity, it leads to dissatisfaction, demotivation, and potentially negative behavior.

Core Concept of Equity Theory

Equity in the workplace refers to the balance between what an employee puts into their job (inputs) and what they get out of it (outcomes). According to the theory:

  • Inputs: The employee’s contributions, including effort, time, skills, education, and loyalty.
  • Outcomes: The rewards the employee receives in return, such as salary, recognition, job security, and promotions.

Adams suggested that individuals often evaluate whether they are being treated fairly by comparing their input-output ratio with that of others, such as co-workers, colleagues in similar positions, or peers from different organizations.

Perception of Fairness vs. Actual Fairness

Fairness is largely subjective. What one person perceives as fair may not seem fair to another.

For example, if Employee A feels they work harder than Employee B but are paid less, they may feel an imbalance between their inputs (effort, time, skills) and outputs (salary, recognition). The feeling of unfair treatment leads to dissatisfaction and demotivation, impacting their productivity.

On the other hand, if Employee B is paid more, but Employee A perceives that Employee B has a higher level of skill or greater contributions, they might not feel the same level of injustice, and may not be demotivated.

Thus, Equity Theory emphasizes how individuals perceive their fairness in comparison with others, rather than an objective measure of fairness or reality. This makes perception of equity crucial to the theory’s understanding.

Key Elements in Equity Theory

Inputs

Inputs are the personal resources or efforts an employee contributes to their role. Inputs can be things like:

  • Time: The hours dedicated to work, including overtime.
  • Effort: The physical, emotional, and cognitive energy spent on tasks.
  • Skills and Abilities: The knowledge, competencies, and capabilities an employee brings to the job.
  • Experience and Education: The level of education or prior work experience influencing their job performance.
  • Loyalty and Commitment: The emotional attachment and long-term dedication to the organization.

Outcomes

These are the rewards or benefits an employee receives in return for their inputs. Outcomes can include:

  • Monetary rewards: Salary, bonuses, and benefits.
  • Non-monetary rewards: Recognition, job satisfaction, and opportunities for advancement.
  • Job security: Stability and long-term employment prospects.
  • Personal rewards: Fulfillment, growth, and sense of accomplishment.

Reference Person/Group

The reference person or group is the individual or group with whom an employee compares themselves to determine fairness in their input-output ratio. This reference group may vary and can include:

  • Other: Another employee, co-worker, or someone in a similar role, either inside or outside the organization.
  • Self: The individual’s past experiences, whether in the same or a different job.
  • System: The established expectations of what employees should receive based on their contract or organizational norms.

Perception of Inequity

When employees compare their input-output ratio to others and feel an imbalance, inequity arises. This can be of two types:

Under-rewarded Inequity

This occurs when employees feel they are contributing more (higher inputs) but receiving less (lower outcomes) compared to others. This can lead to feelings of frustration, resentment, and demotivation.

Example: An employee might feel that they are working harder than a colleague but are receiving the same salary, leading to dissatisfaction.

Over-rewarded Inequity

This occurs when employees feel they are contributing less (lower inputs) but receiving more (higher outcomes) compared to others. This can lead to guilt, anxiety, and a feeling of discomfort, as the employee may feel undeserving of the rewards.

Example: An employee may feel guilty for receiving a salary increase while contributing less than their peers, leading to a reduction in motivation to perform at the same level.

Impact of Perceived Inequity

When inequity is perceived, employees typically experience psychological distress. This distress motivates individuals to take actions to restore fairness. The greater the inequity, the more significant the distress, which leads to increased efforts to restore equity.

For instance, an employee who feels they are under-rewarded may:

  • Reduce their work efforts (decrease inputs).
  • Seek a raise or additional rewards (increase outputs).
  • Change their perception of fairness by comparing themselves with others (alter perceptions of self or others).
  • Leave the organization to find a more equitable environment.

Summary

Equity Theory explains that fairness, as perceived by individuals, plays a key role in motivating people. When employees perceive an imbalance between their inputs and outcomes, whether they feel under-rewarded or over-rewarded, it creates dissatisfaction and motivates them to restore equity. This understanding of motivation is essential for organizations to consider when managing employee rewards, satisfaction, and performance.

This introduction to Equity Theory sets the foundation for understanding how fairness and perceptions of justice drive motivation in the workplace, which can ultimately affect an organization's success. By ensuring employees feel their contributions are fairly rewarded, organizations can foster a more motivated, productive, and satisfied workforce.

5. Application of Equity Theory

Equity theory has broad applications in various workplace settings and can significantly influence employee behavior, job satisfaction, and organizational performance. The theory is particularly useful in understanding how employees react when they perceive inequity in the distribution of rewards and resources. Below, we discuss the different behaviors that can emerge due to perceived inequity and how these behaviors affect organizational outcomes.

Effects of Perceived Inequity:

When individuals perceive that there is a disparity between their inputs and the outcomes they receive compared to others, it can lead to various behaviors. These reactions are often designed to reduce the discomfort caused by inequity. Adams theorized that the intensity of the reaction is directly proportional to the level of perceived inequity.

  • Lower Job Satisfaction: When employees feel their contributions (inputs) are not adequately rewarded (outputs), it can lead to dissatisfaction. This sense of unfairness reduces job satisfaction, making employees feel undervalued.
  • Loss of Motivation: Employees may become less motivated to contribute their best efforts. For example, if an employee believes that the effort they put in is not rewarded as much as their peers, they may reduce their work output.
  • Deviant Workplace Behavior: Employees may engage in behaviors such as absenteeism, lateness, or sabotage as a form of retaliation against perceived injustice.
  • Low Employee Morale: Perceptions of inequity can significantly harm employee morale, creating a negative atmosphere in the workplace.

The Process of Restoring Equity:

According to equity theory, individuals who perceive inequity will be motivated to restore balance. There are two primary methods for restoring equity:

  • Actual Restoration: This involves making tangible changes, such as requesting a raise or reducing work output to restore balance.
  • Psychological Restoration: When external changes are not possible, individuals may distort their perceptions to justify the situation internally.

Practical Applications in Organizations:

To prevent perceived inequities, organizations should consider the following:

  • Fairness in Compensation: Regular pay audits can help identify compensation disparities that may lead to dissatisfaction.
  • Recognition and Reward Systems: Ensuring that employees feel their contributions are fairly acknowledged can help reduce feelings of inequity.
  • Job Roles and Responsibility Distribution: Managers should balance workloads to avoid perceptions of inequity.
  • Career Advancement and Promotions: Transparent promotion practices can help avoid feelings of unfairness in career progression.
  • Employee Feedback Mechanisms: Regular feedback can identify and address potential inequities early on.

In conclusion, understanding and addressing perceived inequities is crucial for maintaining a motivated, satisfied, and productive workforce.

6. Inequality Reduction Methods

When employees perceive inequity, they may try to restore balance using several methods. Here are the primary methods of inequity reduction or equity restoration that individuals may adopt:

  • Person Altering His Inputs: Individuals may increase or decrease their contributions (effort, time, resources) based on perceived inequity. For example, an employee may put in more effort if they feel under-rewarded or reduce their inputs if they feel over-rewarded.
  • Person Altering His Outputs: Employees may seek to increase or decrease their rewards to restore balance. This could include requesting a raise, asking for more recognition, or reducing their expectations of rewards.
  • Person Altering Perceptions of Self: Employees may change how they perceive their own contributions. This could involve convincing themselves that their efforts are more valuable than they initially thought.
  • Person Altering Perceptions of Others: Employees may change their views about the efforts and rewards of others. This could involve minimizing others’ inputs or justifying the rewards others receive.
  • Change the Situation: If individuals cannot restore equity through the other methods, they may choose to change their job or even leave the organization.
  • Change Comparisons: Employees may shift their comparison group, comparing themselves to others who are in a less favorable situation to justify their own position.

In conclusion, organizations should focus on maintaining fairness and transparency to help prevent the negative consequences of perceived inequity. By doing so, they can foster a more productive and harmonious work environment.
