Lesson 6: Roles of a Manager

Objectives

  • Classify managerial roles into categories.
  • Explore the three main roles of managers: interpersonal, informational, and decisional.
  • Provide activities that help managers execute these roles effectively.

Introduction

Managers in educational settings, such as school principals and department heads, play a critical role in organizational success. Their multifaceted roles influence job satisfaction, resource optimization, and overall educational outcomes.

Key Theories about Managerial Roles:

  • Fayol’s Theory: Focuses on planning, organizing, and controlling.
  • Behaviorists: Emphasize team building.
  • Management Scientists: Highlight decision-making.
  • Contingency School: Emphasizes interactions with external environments.

Classification of Managerial Roles

Managerial roles are classified into four categories, based on Henry Mintzberg’s framework (1971) and Coulson’s addition (1987):

  • Interpersonal Roles: Building relationships.
  • Informational Roles: Handling and sharing information.
  • Decisional Roles: Making strategic decisions.
  • Leading Professional Roles: Emphasizes leadership in professional development.

Roles of a Manager

1. Interpersonal Role

This role revolves around human interactions necessary for achieving organizational goals. Sub-roles include:

  • Figurehead: Symbolic and ceremonial tasks. Example: Greeting visitors and signing documents.
  • Leader: Motivating and mentoring employees. Example: Holding staff meetings and evaluating performance.
  • Liaison: Networking and communication. Example: Attending parent meetings and fostering external relationships.

2. Informational Role

Managers handle the flow of information within and outside the organization. Sub-roles include:

  • Monitor: Gathering and assessing information. Example: Conducting performance reviews and staying updated on trends.
  • Disseminator: Sharing information internally. Example: Explaining performance goals to staff.
  • Spokesperson: Representing the organization externally. Example: Publicizing achievements through brochures.

3. Decisional Role

This role involves using information to make strategic decisions. Sub-roles include:

  • Entrepreneur: Driving innovation and change. Example: Integrating ICT into teaching methods.
  • Disturbance Handler: Resolving crises. Example: Mediating a teacher strike.
  • Resource Allocator: Optimizing resources. Example: Allocating books and equipment.
  • Negotiator: Facilitating agreements. Example: Mediating between staff and management during disputes.

4. Leading Professional Role

This role emphasizes leadership in educational contexts, including:

  • Goal Setter and Evaluator: Defining organizational goals and monitoring progress.
  • Curriculum Coordinator: Ensuring curriculum alignment with objectives.
  • Teacher: Occasionally stepping into teaching roles when needed.
  • Exemplar of Values: Modeling professional behaviors like punctuality and commitment.

Conclusion

The roles outlined demonstrate that a manager’s responsibilities are dynamic and interconnected. By performing interpersonal, informational, decisional, and leading professional roles effectively, managers like Mr. Kulkarni inspire a collaborative and high-performing environment, fostering educational excellence and organizational success.
