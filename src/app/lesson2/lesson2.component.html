Lesson 2: Concept of Management with Special Reference to Education

1.1 Introduction

Institutions such as schools, hospitals, and government offices are established to address societal needs. However, simply having these institutions is not sufficient for them to succeed. Proper management is essential for ensuring that these institutions achieve their goals effectively. In the case of a school, effective management involves proper planning, organization of resources, directing staff, and controlling activities to ensure the smooth operation of the school. Management ensures that teachers, students, and materials are well-organized, and the environment is conducive to learning.

In education, good management is the backbone of success. It involves four key functions: planning (determining what needs to be done), organizing (arranging resources), directing (guiding people), and controlling (monitoring and making adjustments as needed). Furthermore, management in education plays a significant role in linking schools to society. Schools help shape future citizens who will contribute to the economy and societal growth, and thus schools must adapt to societal changes by continuously updating teaching methods and curriculums.

1.2 Concept of Management

Management can be understood in three different ways: as a noun (the people in charge), as a process (the steps taken to achieve goals), and as a discipline (a field of study).

Management as a Noun

When referring to management as a noun, we talk about the people responsible for overseeing the institution. These individuals are tasked with making decisions, guiding staff, and ensuring the institution runs effectively. In a school, the principal, department heads, and teachers are considered part of the management team.

Management as a Process

Management is also a process that includes a series of actions taken by the leadership to ensure the institution achieves its goals. The key steps in this process are:

  • Planning: Establishing clear objectives, like setting goals for the school year or deciding the curriculum.
  • Organizing: Arranging the necessary resources, such as classrooms, materials, and staff.
  • Staffing: Ensuring that the right people are in the right roles.
  • Delegating: Assigning tasks to others within the institution.
  • Controlling: Monitoring progress and making necessary adjustments to ensure that everything is going as planned.

Management as a Discipline

As a field of study, management focuses on learning the theories, principles, and practices that are essential for becoming an effective leader or manager. In education, those studying educational management learn about leadership, decision-making, and strategies for improving the institution’s performance.

1.3 Meaning of Management of Education

The management of education refers to the oversight and guidance of educational activities within an institution. This involves ensuring that the school or college fulfills its educational objectives and meets the needs of students, staff, and society. Education management is similar to managing any other organization, but it focuses specifically on educational functions such as teaching, learning, and research.

Management as a Noun

In the context of education, management refers to the individuals responsible for running the institution, including the principal, department heads, and teachers. They make decisions, set goals, and ensure that all educational activities run smoothly.

Management as a Process

The management process in education follows similar steps as those in any other organization. It includes:

  • Planning: Setting educational goals and designing the curriculum.
  • Organizing: Ensuring that resources, like classrooms and teachers, are in place to carry out the plan.
  • Staffing: Hiring qualified teachers, administrators, and support staff.
  • Delegating: Assigning specific tasks to staff members.
  • Controlling: Monitoring progress and making adjustments when necessary.

Management as a Discipline

Educational management as a discipline focuses on understanding theories related to leadership, motivation, and organizational behavior to help manage schools and educational institutions effectively. These theories guide decision-making and the management of resources, ensuring that the school or institution functions efficiently.

The management of education plays a key role in improving the quality of education and adapting to the ever-changing needs of society. It also involves reaching out to the community and ensuring that educational institutions contribute to the development of the economy and society. Education managers must be aware of societal changes and adjust teaching methods, curriculum, and educational practices accordingly to keep the education system relevant and effective. Effective educational management is critical to ensuring that students receive a quality education, and that schools and colleges remain adaptable to societal shifts.

Management Concepts - Detailed Explanation

1.4 Development of the Concept of Management

The concept of management has evolved over time, with various scholars contributing to its development. The views on management differ, especially in terms of its functions, its relationship with administration, and its application in various fields such as education, business, and government. This section discusses how the concept of management has evolved through different thinkers.

Views on Administration and Management

  • Management and Administration are the Same (Fayol, Koontz, and O'Donnell):

    Henri Fayol, a French management theorist, along with Koontz and O'Donnell, viewed management and administration as essentially the same. Both terms are used interchangeably, and both involve the same basic functions—planning, organizing, staffing, directing, and controlling.

    Example: In an educational setting, whether it's a school principal or a department head, the tasks of planning lessons, organizing resources, and managing budgets are the same, with the only difference being the level of decision-making.

  • Administration and Management are Different (Newman, Sheldon, Spriegel, Milward, Lansberg):

    Other theorists like Newman proposed that administration and management are distinct. Administration involves strategic decision-making, policy formulation, and long-term planning, while management focuses on executing these policies on a day-to-day basis.

    Example: In a school, the government or education department at the national level sets policies for curriculum or teacher standards, while the principal and teachers implement those policies.

  • Management is Part of Administration (Brech):

    Brech suggested that management is a subset of administration. While administration sets policies, management implements them and ensures resources are effectively used to execute those policies. Management also provides feedback to administration on the effectiveness of the policies.

    Example: National educational policies are set by the government (administration), but school principals and teachers are responsible for implementing them.

Peter Drucker’s Perspective on Management

Peter Drucker, a renowned management theorist, introduced the concepts of efficiency and effectiveness as two key measures of management performance:

  • Efficiency: Doing things right—completing tasks with the least amount of resources used.
  • Effectiveness: Doing the right things—ensuring the chosen goals and objectives are the correct ones to pursue.

Drucker’s ideas also align with the systems approach to management, which views organizations as systems composed of inputs (resources), processes (activities), and outputs (results). Efficiency and effectiveness should be balanced to achieve optimal outcomes.

Example: In education, efficiency might be reflected in the number of students passing exams, while effectiveness looks at whether students are gaining essential life skills and critical thinking abilities.

1.5 Levels of Management

Understanding the levels of management in an organization is essential for grasping how decisions are made and how goals are achieved. Management is typically divided into three levels: top-level, middle-level, and lower-level.

Top-Level Management

Definition: This is the highest level of management and is responsible for setting the overall direction and vision of the organization. These individuals make strategic decisions that affect the long-term success of the organization.

Responsibilities:

  • Policy formulation and strategic decision-making.
  • Allocating resources effectively.
  • Providing leadership and overseeing the organization’s overall performance.

Example: In a school, top-level management would include the school board or the education department at the national or state level, responsible for setting educational policies and goals.

Middle-Level Management

Definition: Middle-level management acts as a bridge between top-level management and lower-level management. They are responsible for implementing the broad policies set by top management and ensuring that the organization’s goals are achieved.

Responsibilities:

  • Translating top management’s strategic goals into actionable plans.
  • Supervising and coordinating the work of different departments.
  • Ensuring that resources are used efficiently to meet the organization’s objectives.

Example: In a school, the principal is considered middle management, as they implement policies set by the school board and manage day-to-day activities.

Lower-Level Management

Definition: This level focuses on directly supervising the work of employees and ensuring that tasks are performed according to plan.

Responsibilities:

  • Assigning tasks, supervising staff, and solving operational problems.
  • Reporting progress to middle management.
  • Ensuring that work is completed in accordance with policies and standards.

Example: In a school, teachers represent lower-level management. They directly interact with students and manage day-to-day classroom activities.

Relativity of Management Levels

The terms "top," "middle," and "lower" levels are relative. Depending on the organization’s structure, the levels may differ. For example, at the national education level, the government or education ministry might be considered top-level management.

How the Levels Work Together

  • Top-level management sets strategic goals and policies.
  • Middle-level management translates these into specific plans and coordinates actions.
  • Lower-level management ensures that day-to-day tasks are completed.

Effective communication between these levels is crucial to ensure organizational success.

1.6 Summary

The summary recaps the main ideas about management and its application in education, focusing on key takeaways:

  • Connotations of ‘Management’: Management can be seen as a role (managers), a process (the functions of management), or a discipline (the body of knowledge on managing organizations).
  • Management of Education: The management of educational institutions follows the same principles as general management but applies them specifically to teaching, learning, and resource management.
  • Development of Management Concepts: Theories about management have evolved. Some theorists see administration and management as the same, while others distinguish between them. The relationship between the two is often intertwined, with administration focusing on policy and management focusing on execution.
  • Efficiency vs. Effectiveness: Efficiency refers to doing tasks well with minimal resources, while effectiveness refers to choosing the right tasks that contribute to the organization’s overall goals.
  • Levels of Management: Management is divided into three levels—top, middle, and lower—each with distinct responsibilities. These levels must work together to ensure the organization’s objectives are met.

The summary emphasizes the interconnectedness of these concepts and how they contribute to the effective management of educational institutions.
