Lesson 17: Controlling as a Function of Management

1.0 Objectives

By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to:

  • Explain the steps involved in controlling – Understand the stages through which managers monitor, evaluate, and correct organizational activities.
  • Understand the importance of controlling in management – Recognize how controlling ensures that the set goals are met effectively and efficiently.
  • Establish the relationship between controlling and directing – Understand how controlling complements directing to ensure smooth functioning of the organization.
  • Discuss the need and importance of directing – Learn about the role of directing in motivating, guiding, and leading employees to achieve organizational goals.

1.1 Introduction

Controlling is one of the essential functions of management that ensures an organization’s objectives are met effectively and efficiently. While planning sets the direction, and organizing allocates resources, controlling ensures that the set plans are executed successfully. It involves monitoring and evaluating the performance of activities and taking corrective actions whenever deviations are noticed.

In any organization, there are performance standards that must be achieved. However, performance may not always align with these expectations. When discrepancies occur, controlling helps identify these gaps and provides necessary corrective actions. The process of controlling also helps in optimizing resource usage, ensuring proper processes are followed, and ultimately achieving the desired outcomes.

In essence, controlling acts as a feedback mechanism that ensures all activities remain aligned with the organization’s goals. Without effective control, operations can drift off course, resources may be misused, and the set goals may not be achieved.

Moreover, controlling provides insights into areas needing improvement, leading to better strategic decisions, and more efficient organizational functioning. It is tightly connected with other functions like planning and organizing, ensuring that the organization’s efforts stay aligned with its objectives.

1.2 Steps in Controlling

Controlling is a systematic and structured process that follows certain steps to ensure performance aligns with organizational goals. These steps help managers identify any deviations and take corrective measures to get back on track.

1. Establishment of Controlled Standards

The first step in the controlling process is to set specific standards or benchmarks against which actual performance will be measured. These standards must be clear, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART). The standards should reflect key aspects like quantity, quality, cost, and time.

For instance, a company that aims to improve customer satisfaction might set a standard of achieving 90% positive feedback on customer surveys within the next quarter.

2. Measurement of Performance

After setting the standards, the next step is to measure actual performance. This involves collecting reliable and relevant data to assess whether activities are being completed as planned. Accurate performance data is essential for making informed decisions during the control process.

In the case of a school, this measurement could include tracking student performance through grades, attendance, or even participation in extracurricular activities.

3. Comparison between Performance and Set Standards

Once the performance data is collected, it is compared to the established standards. This comparison helps identify any discrepancies between what was expected and the actual performance. If actual performance meets or exceeds the standards, no action is necessary. However, if deviations are found, further analysis is needed to understand the cause.

For example, if a company set a sales target of $100,000 for the quarter but achieved only $85,000, the manager would investigate why the sales did not meet the target.

4. Correction of Deviations from Standards (Corrective Action)

If deviations are found, corrective actions are needed to address the gap between the desired and actual performance. These actions could involve adjusting strategies, reallocating resources, or changing processes to ensure performance is back on track.

In a school, if students are falling behind in their grades, corrective actions might include extra tutoring, revising teaching strategies, or increasing parent-teacher communication.

5. Communicating the Discrepancy

It is essential to communicate any discrepancies between expected and actual performance to the relevant parties in the organization. This ensures everyone understands the situation and is on the same page regarding the steps to address the issues.

For example, if a manufacturing unit is not meeting its production targets, it is important to communicate this to all relevant teams so that corrective actions, such as adjusting production schedules or increasing staffing, can be implemented.

6. Implementation of Corrective Plans

The final step is to implement the corrective actions that have been decided. This involves putting the necessary changes into effect and continuously monitoring their impact. The implementation of corrective plans should be followed by ongoing performance measurement to ensure that the organization is back on track.

For instance, after providing additional training to teachers to enhance their teaching methods, the organization must observe whether student performance improves in the subsequent evaluations.

1.3 Importance of Controlling

Controlling is crucial for ensuring that organizational goals are met in the most efficient and effective manner possible. Below are several reasons why controlling is so important:

  • Adjustments in Operations: Controlling enables managers to make timely adjustments to operations based on performance data. This ensures that if anything goes off-track, corrective measures are put in place to get things back on course.
  • Policy Verification: Through controlling, managers can evaluate whether the policies and strategies put in place are yielding the desired results. By comparing actual performance against standards, it becomes clear whether current strategies are effective or need to be revised.
  • Coordination of Activities: Controlling ensures that all departments within an organization work in sync. Proper coordination helps eliminate redundancies, avoids overlaps in tasks, and ensures that resources are optimally utilized.
  • Monitoring Environmental Changes: Controlling helps organizations stay adaptable by monitoring changes in the external environment. This includes technological advances, economic changes, new regulations, or evolving social trends.
  • Enhancing Organizational Efficiency and Effectiveness: Through regular performance measurement and corrective actions, managers can ensure that resources are used optimally, and processes are running smoothly, leading to improved results.

1.4 Directing

Directing is the management function that involves leading, guiding, and motivating employees to perform their tasks effectively and efficiently to meet the organization’s goals. It ensures that employees are engaged, motivated, and focused on their roles.

Definition of Directing

Directing is the process of providing leadership, guidance, and motivation to employees to ensure that tasks are completed effectively and aligned with organizational goals. It involves clear communication, providing resources, motivating employees, and supervising performance.

Key Aspects of Directing:

  • Motivating Employees: Motivating employees is central to directing. By understanding individual needs, managers can provide incentives (both intrinsic and extrinsic) that keep employees engaged and productive.
  • Leadership and Supervision: Directing requires strong leadership and supervision. Effective leaders guide their teams by setting clear expectations, providing regular feedback, and making adjustments when necessary.
  • Communication: Clear communication is essential in directing. Managers must clearly communicate tasks, goals, and expectations. Regular updates and feedback help ensure employees stay informed and focused on their objectives.
  • Providing Guidance: Managers need to support employees by offering training, mentorship, and problem-solving assistance to help them overcome challenges and perform their best.

1.5 Elements of Direction

Effective direction involves several key elements that work together to ensure tasks are completed efficiently:

  • Communication – Ensures that tasks, roles, and expectations are clearly understood.
  • Leadership and Supervision – Guides employees through strong leadership and effective supervision.
  • Motivation – Drives employee performance by understanding their needs and providing appropriate incentives.
  • Feedback and Recognition – Regularly assesses performance and provides positive reinforcement and constructive criticism.
  • Coordination – Ensures that all departments and teams work together toward common goals.
  • Delegation – Assigns tasks to the most suitable individuals, empowering them to take ownership and responsibility.

Conclusion

In conclusion, both controlling and directing are crucial for the success of an organization. While controlling ensures that performance aligns with set standards, directing ensures that employees are motivated, guided, and equipped to carry out their tasks effectively. Together, these functions help organizations achieve their goals efficiently and stay on track, even in the face of challenges.

1.6 Need and Importance of Direction

Direction plays a vital role in ensuring that an organization operates smoothly and achieves its goals efficiently. It is one of the key functions of management and is essential for the effective implementation of plans and strategies. Direction not only helps guide employees but also creates an environment that fosters growth, motivation, and collaboration. Below are the key reasons for the need and importance of direction in any organization:

  • Leader Initiates Communication: Effective direction starts with leadership. The leader is responsible for initiating communication within the organization. Communication is the foundation of all management processes, and without it, employees will not have the necessary information to carry out tasks.
  • Motivates People: Direction involves motivating employees to achieve higher levels of performance. Motivation can be intrinsic (coming from within the individual) or extrinsic (external rewards). A key element of direction is providing the necessary guidance, encouragement, and rewards to motivate employees.
  • Develops Teams: Direction helps in the formation and development of effective teams within the organization. A manager’s role in directing is not only to guide individuals but also to create a sense of teamwork. By fostering collaboration and cohesion, the manager ensures that all team members work towards common goals.
  • Boosts Morale: One of the significant benefits of direction is that it boosts the morale of employees. When employees receive clear direction, support, and recognition, their confidence grows, leading to increased enthusiasm and a positive attitude toward the organization.
  • Ensures Proper Use of Resources: Direction ensures that the resources of an organization—whether human, financial, or material—are used effectively and efficiently. By guiding employees on how to utilize resources properly, a manager can ensure that organizational goals are met without wastage.
  • Helps in Achieving Organizational Goals: The ultimate goal of direction is to ensure that the objectives and goals of the organization are achieved. By providing clear guidance, feedback, and motivation, the manager helps employees focus on tasks that align with the organization’s goals.

In Conclusion: Direction is essential for the overall success of an organization. It helps align individual efforts with organizational goals, motivates employees, improves morale, and ensures the efficient use of resources.

1.7 Control: Monitoring and Evaluation in the Process of Management

Control is a crucial function in the management process, ensuring that the organization's activities align with its goals and that corrective actions are taken when necessary. Without control, it would be difficult to ensure that all efforts are directed toward achieving organizational objectives. Control allows managers to monitor progress, identify issues, and take timely actions to keep the organization on track. This section will delve into the importance of control, the process of control, and real-life examples of how it works in practice.

1. Definition of Control

Control in management refers to the process of setting standards, measuring actual performance, comparing this performance with the established standards, and taking corrective actions when needed. This helps ensure that the organization's objectives are being met effectively and efficiently. Control is often described as a feedback loop, where the outcomes of actions are assessed and adjustments are made to improve future performance.

2. Importance of Control in Management

  • Ensures Goal Achievement: Control ensures that all efforts are aligned with the organization’s goals. It helps track whether activities are moving toward achieving these goals and allows for adjustments when they are not.
  • Reduces Risk: By monitoring progress, managers can identify potential problems early, reducing the risk of failure. Early identification of discrepancies can prevent small issues from escalating into major problems.
  • Improves Performance: Control helps identify areas where performance is lagging and allows managers to take corrective actions to improve performance. This continuous improvement leads to higher efficiency and productivity.
  • Enhances Accountability: With clear standards and performance measures in place, individuals and teams are more accountable for their actions. They are aware that their performance will be monitored, which motivates them to work more effectively.
  • Ensures Resource Optimization: Control ensures that resources—whether financial, human, or material—are used effectively and efficiently. By monitoring their usage, control helps prevent wastage and ensures resources are allocated where they are most needed.

3. The Control Process

The control process involves several key steps that work together to ensure effective monitoring and correction of actions. These steps are:

  • Setting Standards: The first step in control is setting standards or benchmarks against which actual performance will be measured.
  • Measuring Actual Performance: After setting standards, the next step is to measure actual performance. This involves collecting data and information to assess how well the organization or individual is performing relative to the standards.
  • Comparing Actual Performance with Standards: Once performance is measured, it is compared to the standards set earlier. This comparison helps identify whether the performance is on track or deviating from the desired outcomes.
  • Taking Corrective Actions: When deviations from the set standards are found, corrective actions must be taken to bring performance back in line with the goals.
  • Feedback Loop: The final step in the control process is ensuring that feedback is provided to employees, teams, or individuals.

4. Types of Control

  • Preventive Control: Focuses on preventing problems before they arise.
  • Concurrent Control: Takes place in real-time, allowing managers to monitor performance as it happens and make adjustments immediately.
  • Corrective Control: Comes into play when performance deviates from the established standards, involving corrective actions to address discrepancies and improve performance.

5. Real-Life Examples of Control in Action

  • In Schools: A school’s performance management system involves continuous control through regular assessments of students' academic progress.
  • In Manufacturing: A factory manager monitors the production line's output and compares it with the set production targets.
  • In Healthcare: A hospital administrator tracks patient care metrics, such as wait times and recovery rates, and compares them with quality standards.

6. Challenges in Control

While control is essential, it is not always easy to implement effectively. Some common challenges include resistance to change, measurement difficulties, and the cost of monitoring.

Conclusion: Control is a vital aspect of management that ensures the organization stays aligned with its goals. Through careful planning, measuring, comparing, and adjusting performance, managers can maintain progress and address problems before they escalate.
