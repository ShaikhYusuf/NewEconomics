Lesson 38: Intervention Techniques for Organizational Development

1. Introduction (Detailed Explanation)

In the dynamic environment of modern organizations, growth and development depend heavily on how effectively a company manages its internal processes and adapts to external changes. One of the primary ways organizations strive for improvement is through intervention techniques, which serve as change agents aimed at enhancing productivity, performance, and overall organizational health.

Interventions, in the context of organizational development (OD), refer to purposeful activities designed to bring about change, improvement, and optimization within the organization's structure, culture, and operations. These interventions are not merely spontaneous actions but are strategic, structured efforts that align the organization’s internal environment with its desired goals.

The goal of interventions is to address and resolve issues that hinder an organization's performance, behavior, or efficiency by introducing systemic changes. An intervention can take several forms, including one-on-one coaching, team development exercises, workshops, structured group discussions, and feedback mechanisms. These actions help diagnose organizational problems, create new methods or processes for solving them, and ensure sustainable improvements.

Effective organizational development relies on selecting the appropriate intervention technique and executing it properly. For example, interventions like Process Consultation, Survey Feedback, Sensitivity Training, and Management by Objectives (MBO) each serve distinct purposes and are chosen based on the organizational context, the specific problems being faced, and the desired outcomes.

2. Process Consultation

Process Consultation is a concept that was first articulated in Edgar Schein's 1969 book titled Process Consultation. Schein, a professor at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology’s Sloan School of Management, defines Process Consultation as “a philosophy of ‘helping,’ and a technology or methodology of how to be helpful.”

The primary goal of Process Consultation is not to provide direct solutions to problems but to assist organizations in identifying their problems and facilitating the discovery of their own solutions. The consultant plays the role of a facilitator who helps the client understand the dynamics of the organization and how to improve them.

  • Communication: Examining how communication flows within the organization.
  • Functional Roles of Members: Identifying and analyzing roles and responsibilities within the organization.
  • Group Problem-Solving and Decision Making: Observing how teams make decisions and address issues collectively.
  • Group Norms and Growth: Understanding the norms and expectations that guide group behavior.
  • Leadership and Authority: Analyzing the leadership styles employed in the organization.
  • Inter-group Cooperation and Competition: Examining the dynamics between different teams or departments in the organization.

3. Survey Feedback

Survey Feedback is an organizational intervention method developed by Mann and his associates between 1957 and 1965. It helps organizations understand and address critical issues by collecting data, analyzing it, and encouraging action based on the feedback provided.

  • Data Collection: Gathering feedback through structured questionnaires.
  • Feedback of Information: Sharing results with the organization in oral or written form.
  • Developing Action Plans: Creating action plans based on survey findings.
  • Follow-Up: Assessing the effectiveness of action plans and ensuring sustainable improvements.

4. Sensitivity Training

Sensitivity Training is an approach that focuses on making individuals more aware of their own prejudices, biases, and emotions, as well as developing greater empathy for others. This training is particularly important in modern workplaces where people come from various backgrounds, cultures, and identities.

  • Objective: To increase awareness of biases and prejudices among employees.
  • Focus: Improving interpersonal relationships and understanding across diverse groups.

Objective of Sensitivity Training

Promote Self-Awareness: Sensitivity Training helps individuals become aware of their internal biases, judgments, and reactions that might affect their interactions with others. This self-awareness is essential to recognizing how one’s actions and attitudes impact others in a group setting or workplace.

Foster Empathy: One of the key goals is to develop empathy, which is the ability to understand and share the feelings of others. This includes recognizing and respecting the differences in others' perspectives, which is particularly important in diverse settings where there may be differences in cultural backgrounds, gender, ethnicity, or abilities.

Improve Group Dynamics: Sensitivity Training improves group dynamics by helping people understand how they interact within a group and how group members influence one another. It focuses on developing better communication skills, teamwork, and conflict resolution strategies, ultimately enhancing the cohesion and effectiveness of teams.

Navigate Diversity and Inclusion Issues: Sensitivity Training helps individuals deal with challenges related to diversity, including issues related to gender, race, sexual orientation, and the differently-abled. By becoming more aware of these differences and how they affect interpersonal relationships, employees are better equipped to foster an inclusive workplace.

Origin of Sensitivity Training

The concept of Sensitivity Training was first developed by Kurt Lewin, a German-American psychologist. Lewin conducted a series of change experiments in 1946 for the Connecticut State Interracial Commission, which laid the groundwork for Sensitivity Training. He emphasized the importance of group dynamics and how relationships between individuals in a group could impact the larger social structure.

Sensitivity Training was first used by the National Training Laboratories (NTL) in Bethel, USA, in the form of "T-group training." The term "T-group" refers to training groups where participants engage in open discussion and feedback to learn about themselves and each other. Since its inception, Sensitivity Training has been widely used in organizational development to improve interpersonal relations and organizational culture.

Procedure of Sensitivity Training

Unfreezing the Old Values

The first step in Sensitivity Training involves "unfreezing" or challenging participants' existing attitudes, beliefs, and values. Trainees are encouraged to confront and recognize the inadequacies of their old values, particularly when faced with situations where those values no longer serve them. This is typically done in an unstructured group setting where participants may feel confusion, leading them to explore their assumptions and values.

Development of New Values

After recognizing the limitations of their old values, participants work to develop new ones through group discussions and feedback. This process fosters personal growth as individuals adopt new behaviors and values in line with the group's evolving goals and dynamics.

Refreezing the New Values

The final step involves "refreezing" or solidifying the new values and behaviors. By providing repeated opportunities to practice new behaviors in real-life situations, the newly adopted behaviors become ingrained and permanent, reducing biases and prejudices over time.

Impact of Sensitivity Training

Self-Impact: Sensitivity Training increases self-awareness, emotional intelligence, and interpersonal competence. Participants become better at managing emotions and handling relationships, leading to more respectful, productive, and collaborative interactions.

Role Impact: The training improves participants' understanding of their roles within the organization and enhances their relationships with others, fostering a more collaborative and inclusive environment.

Organizational Impact: Sensitivity Training leads to improved team dynamics, better conflict resolution, and increased organizational effectiveness by fostering a culture of empathy, respect, and collaboration.

Conclusion

Sensitivity Training is a powerful tool for organizational development. It helps individuals improve their interpersonal skills, develop empathy, and navigate diversity, ultimately enhancing teamwork and organizational success. When implemented effectively, it creates a more inclusive, supportive, and productive workplace.
