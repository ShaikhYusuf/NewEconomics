Lesson 7: Skills of Managers

Objectives of the Lesson

  • Understand the skills required to be an effective manager.
  • Learn how these skills are developed over time.

Introduction

Definition of Skills: Skills are abilities or proficiencies developed through education, experience, and practice. Some skills may be natural talents, but many can be acquired and improved over time.

In educational management, effective leadership depends on the specific skills required at different levels of responsibility (e.g., teachers, supervisors, principals). Managers must adapt their skills to evolving roles.

Scenarios of Ms. Sunayana Naik

This section illustrates how skills change with managerial responsibilities:

  • As an Assistant Teacher: Focus on teaching skills like lesson planning, communication, and time management. These technical skills are critical at the first-level management stage.
  • As a Supervisor: Focus shifts to leadership, problem-solving, and coordination. A balance of human and technical skills is required.
  • As a Principal: Focus on administration, strategic planning, and policy implementation. Conceptual skills become paramount.

Three-Skill Approach by Robert Katz

  • Technical Skills:
    • Definition: Specialized knowledge and abilities required to perform specific tasks.
    • Examples: Lesson planning, student evaluation, effective teaching.
    • Relevance: Crucial for first-level managers, like teachers or team leaders.
  • Human Skills:
    • Definition: Ability to work effectively with others, including emotional intelligence and teamwork.
    • Examples: Resolving staff conflicts, understanding student needs, liaising with stakeholders.
    • Relevance: Important at all levels of management.
  • Conceptual Skills:
    • Definition: Ability to understand the organization as a whole and make strategic decisions.
    • Examples: Long-term development planning, policy alignment with mission.
    • Relevance: Most important for top-level managers.

Importance of the Three-Skill Approach

  • Selection of Appropriate Personnel: Different management levels require different skill sets, such as technical skills for teachers and conceptual skills for principals.
  • Development of Managerial Skills:
    • Technical Skills: Developed through education and practice.
    • Human Skills: Fostered through training programs like emotional intelligence workshops.
    • Conceptual Skills: Enhanced through strategic thinking exercises and case studies.

Examples of Skill Applications

  • Scenario Four: Principal fails to consult staff, highlighting the need for human skills.
  • Scenario Five: Teacher supports a distressed student, showcasing empathy and human skills.
  • Scenario Nine: Principal creates a phased development plan, demonstrating conceptual skills.

Relative Importance of Skills

  • First-Level Managers: Emphasis on technical skills.
  • Middle-Level Managers: Balance of technical and human skills.
  • Top-Level Managers: Strong emphasis on conceptual skills.

Summary

Managers at different levels require varying combinations of technical, human, and conceptual skills. As responsibilities increase, the emphasis shifts from operational (technical) to strategic (conceptual) skills. Recognizing these distinctions helps focus skill development for better performance.
